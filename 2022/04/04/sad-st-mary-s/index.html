Sad St Mary's

2022-04-04

Hello there. If you participated in the NCAA tournament this year, chances are, you probably didn’t choose St Mary’s to win it all. In fact, I bet most people don’t even know where St Mary’s is located. I sure as heck didn’t.

Alas, St Mary’s is who I inevitably picked to win it all for this year’s 2022 NCAA Bracket Challenge. Yes, the one everyone submits to ESPN. The one that actually has prize money (and horrendous bragging rights) attached.

If you’re wondering why I picked St Mary’s to win it all (over Seton Hall, no less), do keep reading.

There’s a few reasons why you might think I picked St Mary’s:

I don’t follow NCAA basketball.

I went to St Mary’s for education.

I believe in miracles.

I was using a computational algorithm based on advanced statistics that somehow spit out St Mary’s as your 2022 NCAA Men’s Basketball Champion.

If you guess the last one, hooray. Have an air high-five.

So basically, I used a coding programme to run an algorithm and statistics-based formula that I thought would correctly predict the NCAA tourney winner this year.

I ran three different factors:

• PIE, or Player Impact Efficiency (this is a statistic I stole from the NBA that measures … a lot of stuff. The formula for PIE is complicated and long)

This statistic analyses what % of certain calculated game events that team achieve (these statistics are the traditional statistics of Points, Rebounds, Assists, and Turnover Percentage). If their PIE is above 50% then they have a good chance of winning the game. The formula for this statistic is as follows: (PTS + FGM + FTM - FGA - FTA + DREB + (.5 * OREB) + AST + STL + (.5 * BLK) - PF - TO) / (GmPTS + GmFGM + GmFTM - GmFGA - GmFTA + GmDREB + (.5 * GmOREB) + GmAST + GmSTL + (.5 * GmBLK) - GmPF - GmTO).

• Adjusted Offense (a statistic that measures how many points per 100 possessions)

• Adjusted Defense (a statistic that measures how many points against per 100 possesions by the opponent)

You can take a look at my code below:

teamstats <- teamgames %>% group_by(team_short_display_name, season) %>% arrange(game_date) %>% mutate( team_score = ((field_goals_made-three_point_field_goals_made) * 2) + (three_point_field_goals_made*3) + free_throws_made, team_pie_numerator = (team_score + field_goals_made + free_throws_made - field_goals_attempted - free_throws_attempted + defensive_rebounds + (.5 * offensive_rebounds) + assists + steals + (.5 * blocks) - fouls - turnovers), ) %>% ungroup() %>% rename( team_adj_o = adj_o, team_adj_d = adj_d )

Turns out these weren’t the best factors to use when calculating NCAA matchups (or at least the ones this year).

I think where I mostly went wrong was the PIE statistic.

Why? The NCAA is not the NBA.

Because of the complexity of the formula, I believe that PIE is a statistic that really works with teams that either score a lot, have a small margin of error, and have a more consistent play.

I also failed to add in any of the traditional statistic such as Rebounds, Field Goal Percentage, and Shooting Percentage.

There’s not much to say why my brackets performed the way they did, but unfortunately that’s just how the ball rolls sometimes.

Maybe next year I’ll find a way to tweak my predictions and win it all.
